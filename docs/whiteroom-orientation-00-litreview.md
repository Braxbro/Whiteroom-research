# Whiteroom: An Orientation Literature Review

**No prior work directly tests whether freezing one component's KV cache under a compliant component swap preserves prediction accuracy for orthogonal properties — the Whiteroom hypothesis is novel.** However, converging lines of research across KV cache systems, compositional generalization, disentanglement, binding, synthetic benchmarks, and positional encoding all provide partial precedents, theoretical grounding, and important cautions. The closest works are CacheBlend and EPIC (which show KV reuse is viable with minor repair), Tang et al. 2026 (which demonstrates disentangled slot-filler representations in a small transformer), and Chen et al. 2021 (which directly tests role-filler independence in neural memory). The central tension in the literature is between evidence that transformers *can* learn factored representations under the right conditions and evidence that they *default* to superposition and entanglement.

---

## KV cache reuse works, but nobody asks *why*

The systems literature on KV cache efficiency has produced increasingly aggressive reuse strategies, all of which implicitly rely on a form of representation independence — yet none frame it that way. **PagedAttention** (Kwon et al., SOSP 2023) established the baseline: KV cache sharing is strictly prefix-based, invalidating all entries from the first point of token divergence onward. SGLang's **RadixAttention** (Zheng et al., 2023) extends this to multi-call programs via a radix tree over token sequences, but remains fundamentally prefix-matched. Both systems embody the maximally conservative invalidation policy Whiteroom challenges.

Two recent works push well beyond prefix matching. **CacheBlend** (Yao et al., EuroSys 2025, Best Paper) enables reuse of independently precomputed KV caches for non-prefix chunks in RAG scenarios, finding that only **5–18% of tokens require selective recomputation** to restore cross-chunk attention and recover full-prefill accuracy. **EPIC** (Hu et al., 2024) formalizes "Position-Independent Caching" — an explicit analogy to position-independent code — where chunk KV caches are precomputed independently and "linked" at arbitrary positions, achieving **up to 8× TTFT improvement** with negligible quality loss by recomputing only the first ~32 tokens per chunk to fix attention-sink artifacts.

Both findings are encouraging for Whiteroom: if 82–95% of KV entries survive even the harder operation of stitching independently computed chunks at mismatched positions, they should be even more robust under Whiteroom's simpler operation (swapping a *subsequent* component while keeping the prefix frozen, which is mathematically exact under causal masking). However, neither work measures whether *specific semantic properties* of individual components survive reuse — they evaluate global generation quality, not whether orthogonal flags tied to one component degrade when the other changes. This is Whiteroom's distinct contribution.

A notable gap-in-progress: a vLLM GitHub issue (#25672, 2025) discusses "Generalized KV cache reuse" for compositional contexts, noting that a token's key representation "depends on the full preceding context" in general. The community is groping toward the semantic-awareness Whiteroom formalizes.

## Compositional generalization reveals the entanglement problem

The compositional generalization literature provides the behavioral evidence that Whiteroom's representational hypothesis aims to explain. **SCAN** (Lake & Baroni, ICML 2018) showed that sequence-to-sequence models trained on all uses of "walk twice," "run around right," etc. fail catastrophically (near-0% accuracy) when asked to compose "jump twice" from the primitive "jump" — the model entangles verb identity with compositional structure rather than treating them as independent slots. **COGS** (Kim & Linzen, EMNLP 2020) extended this to semantic parsing, finding that transformers achieving **~99% in-distribution accuracy drop to 16–35% on novel compositions**, with high variance across random seeds suggesting any compositional structure learned is fragile.

Crucially, **Csordás et al. (EMNLP 2021)** demonstrated that much of this failure stems from suboptimal architectural choices rather than fundamental limitations. Simple modifications — **relative positional embeddings, proper embedding scaling, longer training** — improved COGS generalization from 35% to 81% and solved the SCAN length split entirely. The implication for Whiteroom: whether a model learns semantically independent component representations likely depends heavily on positional encoding choice and training regime.

The strongest existence proof comes from **Lake & Baroni (Nature 2023)**, who showed that Meta-Learning for Compositionality (MLC) — a training procedure that exposes a standard transformer to constantly permuted compositional episodes — achieves **human-like systematic generalization**. MLC succeeds precisely because training forces the model to treat components as interchangeable, producing representations where one component's encoding is independent of co-occurring components. This is both a positive signal (transformers *can* learn Whiteroom's desired independence) and a warning (they only do so when training explicitly encourages it).

An additional flagged work: **Wiedemer et al. (NeurIPS 2023)** mathematically formalizes compositionality with independent generative components and proves conditions under which models learn compositional representations. Their key finding — that models can reconstruct in-distribution but fail out-of-distribution because they **entangle component representations in later layers even when given disentangled inputs** — directly predicts a layer-dependent effect in Whiteroom's KV cache experiments.

## The superposition question is the central fork in the road

Whether Whiteroom's cache-freezing test succeeds depends on a fundamental question about transformer representations: do they sit in the "factored" regime or the "superposition" regime? **Elhage et al.'s Toy Models of Superposition** (Anthropic, 2022) demonstrates that neural networks facing more features than dimensions pack features into overlapping directions — the superposition phenomenon — causing interference between nominally independent concepts. Their phase diagram predicts that **high-importance, dense features** are more likely to receive orthogonal (factored) representations, while sparse or less important features get packed into superposition. This predicts that Whiteroom results may vary systematically: components with high-frequency, high-importance flags may show clean independence under swap, while rarer flags may suffer from cross-component interference.

On the positive side, **β-VAE** (Higgins et al., ICLR 2017) provides the theoretical framework showing that factored representations *are* achievable via information-bottleneck pressure, and **ROME** (Meng et al., NeurIPS 2022) demonstrates that factual associations in trained GPT models are semi-localized in mid-layer MLPs — editing one fact preserves others. However, subsequent work (Hase et al.) found weak correlation between causal tracing localization and edit success, suggesting the picture is more distributed than ROME implies.

The most directly relevant finding comes from **Tang, Lake, & Jazayeri (PLOS ONE, February 2026)**, who identify the internal circuit enabling compositional generalization in a small encoder-decoder transformer. The circuit uses **disentangled representations of token position (slot) and token identity (content)** — functions operate on positional embeddings whose identity is disentangled from stored values, mirroring symbolic role-filler binding. Activation edits that modify one component's representation predictably steer outputs for that component alone. This is the closest existing demonstration of the exact independence property Whiteroom probes, albeit in a small model on a different task without explicit KV cache freezing.

## Binding theory predicts when independence should hold

The binding and relational reasoning literature provides the theoretical scaffolding for Whiteroom's typed-port design. **Greff, van Steenkiste, & Schmidhuber (2020)** frame the binding problem as the central bottleneck for neural generalization, identifying three stages — segregation, representation, composition — and warning of the **superposition catastrophe** when multiple entities collapse into a single distributed representation. Their framework directly maps to Whiteroom: can the transformer maintain separate representations for components A and B when they bind through typed ports?

**Slot Attention** (Locatello et al., NeurIPS 2020) offers an architectural solution: competitive attention forces slots to bind to different objects, producing exchangeable, permutation-symmetric representations. However, slots are untyped (any slot can bind any object), and the competitive softmax normalization means slot assignments are implicitly coupled — reallocating one slot's attention changes the others'. Whiteroom's typed ports add a structural constraint absent from Slot Attention.

The most pointed empirical finding comes from **Chen et al. (PeerJ, 2021)**, who directly test role-filler binding independence across architectures. Their key result: **only architectures with external memory** (Fast Weights, Differentiable Neural Computer) successfully maintain independent role-filler bindings for arbitrary novel fillers. Standard RNNs and LSTMs fail because they use conjunctive coding that entangles filler representations. This directly predicts that standard transformers — which lack explicit external memory for binding — may struggle with Whiteroom's independence test, though the transformer's attention mechanism (which Chen et al. did not test) may function as a soft external memory that partially solves the problem.

**Relation Networks** (Santoro et al., NeurIPS 2017) separate entity representation from relational computation via pairwise functions, but do not enforce type constraints or test entity independence. A recent theoretical contribution, "Attention as Binding" (Dhayalkar, arXiv 2512.14709, December 2025), interprets transformer attention as a soft vector-symbolic-architecture binding/unbinding operator — queries define roles, values encode fillers — and proposes metrics for measuring role-filler separation, but lacks empirical validation.

## Synthetic domains have been used this way before — but not quite

Whiteroom's strategy of using a clean synthetic domain with known compositional structure to probe internal representations has strong precedents. **CLEVR** (Johnson et al., CVPR 2017) pioneered the paradigm with objects having independent attributes and a CoGenT split testing novel attribute combinations. **BabyAI** (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., ICLR 2019) uses synthetic gridworlds with compositional instructions to test whether models learn reusable modular representations.

The closest philosophical precedent is **Tracr** (Lindner et al., NeurIPS 2023), which compiles human-readable programs into transformer weights, producing models with fully known internal structure. Tracr explicitly studies whether computational variables remain separable in the residual stream or collapse into superposition — the same independence question Whiteroom asks of KV cache representations, but for compiled rather than trained models.

The **CLRS Algorithmic Reasoning Benchmark** (Veličković et al., ICML 2022) provides a complementary precedent: 30 classical algorithms with full execution traces used to test out-of-distribution generalization in graph neural networks and transformers. Its emphasis on whether learned representations transfer to novel problem structures parallels Whiteroom's component-swap generalization test.

A notable additional find: **Bottlenecked Transformers** (2025, arXiv 2505.16950) proposes periodic global rewrites of the KV cache using Information Bottleneck theory, proving theoretically that standard decoder-only transformers are constrained in forming task-optimal sequence representations in their KV cache. This is the only identified work that specifically targets KV cache representations as a site for analyzing and improving reasoning — making it a direct technical neighbor to Whiteroom despite different motivations.

No prior work combines a synthetic domain with typed component binding and explicit tests of KV cache semantic independence under component swapping. This intersection is Whiteroom's unique contribution.

## Positional encoding is Whiteroom's biggest technical headache

The positional encoding literature reveals the most concrete technical challenge for the cache-freezing paradigm. **RoPE** (Su et al., 2024), now dominant in modern LLMs, applies position-dependent rotation matrices to K and Q vectors, meaning **cached K vectors are permanently stamped with their position of origin**. KVLink (Zhu et al., 2025) demonstrates that naively concatenating independently encoded KV caches with mismatched RoPE positions degrades accuracy by **up to 35%** in QA tasks. The good news: rotations can be undone and redone, making positional correction mechanically straightforward.

**ALiBi** (Press et al., ICLR 2022) offers a more cache-friendly alternative: position is injected only as an additive bias at attention-score computation time, leaving K and V vectors position-clean. Under ALiBi, cached KV states for component A are inherently more portable after a B→B' swap. However, the representations still carry implicit contextual information shaped by position-dependent attention patterns during the forward pass.

Beyond explicit positional encoding, **Wu et al. (ICML 2025)** demonstrate that **causal masking alone creates inherent position bias** — earlier tokens accumulate exponentially more computational paths through deeper layers, producing primacy effects independent of any PE scheme. This predicts that later-layer KV caches are more position-contaminated and thus more likely to be invalid after component swaps than earlier-layer ones.

**Prompt Cache** (Gim et al., MLSys 2024) directly confronts the position-dependence problem, introducing schema-based position assignment for reusable prompt modules and showing that LLMs can tolerate discontinuous position IDs as long as each segment's positional encoding is internally consistent. **StreamingLLM** (Xiao et al., ICLR 2024) identifies the attention-sink phenomenon — disproportionate attention to early tokens regardless of content — warning that the first tokens of any component carry outsized structural roles that could be disrupted by component swaps.

For Whiteroom specifically, these findings suggest that if component A forms the prompt prefix (as described), its KV cache is mathematically exact under causal masking — the concern is not positional invalidity but contextual contamination through residual-stream dependencies built up across layers.

## Conclusion: a genuine gap at a well-prepared intersection

Whiteroom sits at the convergence of six active research threads, each of which provides partial motivation but none of which tests the full hypothesis. The literature establishes that:

- **KV cache reuse is viable** with minimal repair (CacheBlend, EPIC), but nobody has measured component-level semantic preservation rather than global quality.
- **Compositional generalization requires factored representations**, which standard transformers learn unreliably (SCAN, COGS) unless training or architecture explicitly encourages independence (MLC, relative PE).
- **Superposition is the default** (Elhage et al.), but important structured features can receive orthogonal representations, especially in small, clean domains (Tang et al. 2026, Tracr).
- **Independent role-filler binding requires architectural support** beyond standard recurrent networks (Chen et al.), though transformer attention may provide sufficient soft external memory.
- **Positional encoding creates real coupling** in KV representations (RoPE stamps, causal-mask primacy bias), but Whiteroom's prefix-based design sidesteps the hardest version of this problem.

The strongest prior signal that Whiteroom's hypothesis could hold comes from Tang et al.'s finding that a trained transformer develops disentangled slot-filler representations for compositional tasks. The strongest caution comes from the superposition literature: factoredness is not free and depends on feature importance, capacity, and training dynamics. The specific experimental design — measuring orthogonal flag prediction accuracy under KV cache freezing with compliant component swaps — appears to be genuinely novel. No identified work tests this, even approximately, in the specific form proposed.
